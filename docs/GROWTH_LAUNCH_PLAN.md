# Kodara Growth & Alpha Launch Plan

This document is the growth and go-to-market companion to `docs/FINAL_SETUP.md`. FINAL_SETUP.md gates whether the product is technically safe to put in front of real money and real users; this document assumes that gate is eventually passed and answers a different question — who we onboard first, how we get them in the door, how the product grows once they're in, and what non-engineering work has to happen before alpha. Where the two documents would overlap (Supabase config, M-Pesa certification, monitoring), this one defers entirely to FINAL_SETUP.md and does not repeat it.

## 1. Onboarding flow

Kodara's web app today has no marketing landing page or signup wizard — `app/components/KodaraApp.tsx` drops a user straight into the workspace and exposes a role switcher (landlord / property manager / tenant) for preview purposes, with `app/components/PortfolioWorkspace.tsx` driving the owner/manager experience and `app/components/TenantPortal.tsx` driving the tenant experience. The onboarding flow below is what needs to wrap around the existing screens for real alpha users; it is sequenced to match the actual modal-driven creation flow already implemented (`ActionModal` in `PortfolioWorkspace.tsx` handles `property`, `unit`, `tenant`, `message`, and `document` creation as discrete steps).

**(a) Owner/manager first touch.** Step 1 is account creation via Supabase Phone Auth (per `FINAL_SETUP.md` section 1.3) — phone number plus OTP, no separate password to forget, which matters because the target user manages property from a phone, not a desktop. Step 2 is a single mandatory action before anything else unlocks: add the first property, using the existing "Add property" modal (name, address, property type — apartment/flat/house/commercial). Step 3 is adding at least one unit under that property (unit name, monthly rent in KES, status) via the "Add unit" modal — this is the natural point to explain that rent figures here drive every downstream collection and reporting screen, so accuracy matters more than completeness on day one. Step 4 is adding the first tenant against a vacant unit (name and phone number only — no tenant-side password creation at this step, which is correct, since the tenant authenticates separately on their own device). Step 5 is M-Pesa payment configuration at the organization level — this is an admin/owner-only step, not exposed in the demo modals, and is the single highest-leverage thing to get right in onboarding: an owner who adds three properties but never finishes M-Pesa setup gets zero value from the product. It should be surfaced as a persistent banner or blocking checklist item, not a buried settings page, until a real STK push has succeeded end to end. Step 6 is the first dashboard view (`PortfolioWorkspace` overview: rent collection health, occupancy, maintenance resolution) — this is the "aha" screen and should be reachable within minutes of signup, even with just one unit and one tenant, so new owners see the product working before they've invested real setup time. The realistic minimum viable session for a new owner is: phone signup, one property, one unit, one tenant invite, M-Pesa shortcode/till linkage. Anything beyond that (full portfolio import, multiple managers, document uploads) should be deferred to a "complete your portfolio" nudge after the first payment has been observed, not forced upfront — bulk data entry before any payoff is the most likely point where a recruited alpha landlord abandons setup.

**(b) Tenant first touch.** The tenant is created by the owner/manager (step 4 above) with just name and phone — the tenant does not self-register from scratch. The first-touch experience for the tenant is therefore an SMS (via the SMS provider configured per FINAL_SETUP.md section 1.3), not an app-store discovery flow: "Your landlord added you to Kodara for [Property] Unit [X]. Verify your phone to view your balance and pay rent: [link]." Step 1 for the tenant is opening that link and completing Phone Auth OTP verification, which binds their phone number to the `tenant.user_id` record the owner already created — this is the step that has to be airtight, since a mismatch here means a tenant either can't log in or, worse, sees another tenant's balance. Step 2 is landing directly on `TenantPortal`'s balance/payment screen showing `outstanding_balance` for their specific unit and lease — no empty state, no extra setup, because the value (see what I owe, pay it) has to be obvious within one screen. Step 3 is the M-Pesa STK push payment flow itself (`Pay with M-Pesa` / STK push UI already built in `TenantPortal.tsx`), which should be attempted by the tenant within the same session as verification if at all possible — time-to-first-payment from invite is the single metric that best predicts whether a tenant onboarding will stick (see Section 4). Step 4, after a successful payment, is a receipt screen referencing the `mpesa_receipt` and a light prompt to explore maintenance requests and messaging — secondary features that matter for retention but shouldn't be pushed before payment because they're not what got the tenant there. Tenants who never receive an SMS, or whose landlord never finishes M-Pesa setup, are dead-ended with no recourse in-app; the support channel (Section 4) needs a clear path for a tenant to self-report "my landlord added me but nothing works."

## 2. Alpha landlord recruitment plan

The target segment is small, individually-owned residential portfolios in Nairobi — specifically owners or family-appointed managers running 1 to 3 apartment blocks or compounds of roughly 10 to 40 units each, in mid-density areas like Kasarani, Ruaka, Kahawa, Rongai, Ngong Road, or South B/C, where unit counts are large enough that manual rent tracking is genuinely painful but small enough that the owner or one manager still personally reconciles M-Pesa messages by hand (typically in a notebook or a shared Excel sheet) rather than already running a paid PMS. This segment is distinct from large institutional landlords (who are likely already evaluating or using an established player) and distinct from single-unit owners (whose pain is too low to justify behavior change). The defining pain point to pitch against is M-Pesa reconciliation: tenants pay rent into a personal till or paybill at inconsistent times and amounts, and the owner/manager spends hours each month manually matching M-Pesa SMS confirmations to tenants and units, with no system of record and no late-payment visibility until a tenant is already months behind.

Realistic acquisition channels, in rough order of expected efficiency for a pre-launch alpha: (1) direct outreach through Kenyan landlord and property-manager WhatsApp/Facebook groups and forums, which is how this segment already coordinates informally — this is low-cost and high-trust if the pitch is specific rather than generic; (2) referrals through real estate agents and caretakers (mlinzi/watchmen and on-site managers are often the ones who'd actually operate the app day to day, and they have relationships across multiple small landlords, making one agent relationship a multi-landlord lead source); (3) a small number of personal/warm-network introductions, which should be prioritized first since alpha users recruited this way are more forgiving of rough edges and more likely to give honest, detailed feedback; (4) very targeted local Facebook/Instagram ads geo-fenced to the named Nairobi suburbs above, used only after the warm-network and group-outreach channels are exhausted, since paid acquisition into an unproven alpha is inefficient. Given that competitors like Nyumba Zetu, RentSawa, Bomahut, and Pangoni already operate in this exact space with M-Pesa reconciliation as a stated feature (see Section 3 for detail), the pitch cannot lead with "M-Pesa integration" as a novelty — it has to lead with a specific differentiator Kodara actually has, most plausibly the AI tenant-scoring/risk-tier feature described in `docs/AI_SCORING.md`, which none of the identified competitors appear to surface, plus a no-cost, white-glove alpha offer: free onboarding done by the team (the owner/manager doesn't have to enter their own portfolio), no subscription fee during alpha, and direct access to the founder for support and feature requests via WhatsApp.

The incentive for alpha participants should be concrete and time-boxed: free use for a fixed period (e.g., 3 months) post-alpha as thanks for early adoption, plus white-glove data entry so the landlord's switching cost is near zero. The concrete alpha target is 15 to 20 landlords/managers representing 150 to 300 units total before considering public launch — large enough to surface real edge cases in M-Pesa reconciliation (split payments, wrong amounts, duplicate callbacks — the exact failure modes FINAL_SETUP.md section 3 already requires testing for) and tenant behavior at a sample size that produces statistically meaningful week-1 metrics, but small enough that the founder/team can personally support every alpha account rather than relying on the support channel infrastructure described in Section 4 to scale prematurely.

## 3. Post-alpha growth strategy

Kodara's core structural advantage is that it sits on a real two-sided network: every tenant who pays rent through the app is a forced touchpoint with their landlord, and every landlord who adopts the app brings their entire existing tenant base along involuntarily (a tenant doesn't choose to use Kodara — their landlord decides and the tenant either complies or pays the old way). This means the primary growth loop is not classic peer referral but landlord-to-landlord referral seeded by tenant-side visibility: a tenant who has a smooth M-Pesa-through-Kodara payment experience at one property, then moves to a new unit run by a different (non-Kodara) landlord, is a natural source of "you should look at this app my old landlord used" word of mouth — this is slower than direct referral but costs nothing to build beyond making sure the receipt/confirmation experience is something a tenant would actually mention unprompted. The faster, intentional loop is a landlord referral program: an existing alpha or paying landlord who refers another landlord (most plausibly someone in their same WhatsApp group, family network, or building) gets a discount or free month, structured the same way the alpha incentive is — the referring landlord already has total intuition for which other landlords are stuck doing manual M-Pesa reconciliation, which makes this a high-quality lead source. A secondary loop worth instrumenting is the property manager/caretaker channel directly: a single caretaker often operates units for multiple property owners, so a referral incentive aimed at managers rather than just owners has multiplicative reach per recruited individual.

On monetization, the market context from comparable Kenyan products gives a workable anchor: RentSawa is free for up to 5 units and charges roughly KES 1,500/month for a Pro plan unlocking up to 50 units, and Nyumba Zetu/Bomahut/Pangoni-style competitors generally price as per-unit or tiered-portfolio SaaS rather than flat fees, reflecting that the buyer (a small landlord) thinks in cost-per-unit-per-month, not cost-per-account. Given Kodara's target segment of 10 to 40-unit portfolios, a tiered structure tracks the market best: a free or near-free tier for very small portfolios (under 5 units) to remove friction for the smallest landlords and seed word of mouth, a mid tier in the same general band as RentSawa's Pro plan (roughly KES 1,000 to 2,500/month, or a small per-unit fee in the KES 30 to 80/unit/month range) for the core target segment, and a custom/negotiated tier for larger multi-property managers. A transaction-fee model (a small percentage or flat fee per M-Pesa rent payment processed) is worth testing in parallel or as an alternative to subscription, since it aligns Kodara's revenue with actual usage rather than seat count and is a familiar mental model in Kenyan fintech (Safaricom's own Daraja fees work this way) — but it should be modeled carefully against Safaricom's own M-Pesa transaction charges so the landlord's all-in cost doesn't become a deterrent for a population already cost-sensitive. The AI tenant-scoring feature in `docs/AI_SCORING.md` is a credible premium add-on once enough real M-Pesa payment history exists per tenant to make scores meaningful (it requires 12 months of history per the weighting table, so it has no value during alpha or early post-alpha and should not be priced or marketed until that data exists).

## 4. Go-to-market launch checklist

This section deliberately excludes anything already covered by `docs/FINAL_SETUP.md` (Supabase, M-Pesa certification, monitoring/backups, release verification commands). What follows is everything else that has to be true before alpha landlords are invited.

Support channel: a single staffed channel is sufficient at alpha scale — WhatsApp Business is the right choice over email or a ticketing tool, since it matches how the target landlord segment already communicates and lets the team respond in minutes rather than a support-ticket SLA. The channel needs one clearly identified human as the escalation owner for payment disputes (a tenant claiming they paid but the ledger shows otherwise), since this is the failure mode most likely to break trust fastest. The same channel should also be the place tenants report "my landlord added me but I can't log in" issues raised in Section 1(b).

Legal essentials: Kodara needs a published privacy policy and terms of service covering Kenyan Data Protection Act (2019) obligations specifically — data minimization, purpose limitation, consent for M-Pesa transaction access (this is already partially designed for in `docs/AI_SCORING.md`'s consent model, which is a good starting point), and a stated data retention period, plus a clear right-to-erasure process. Real legal review by a Kenyan-qualified advocate is required before any of this is published or relied upon — nothing in this document or produced by this process should be treated as binding legal text, and that review should be scheduled as its own task with its own owner before the first alpha invite goes out, not treated as a documentation checkbox.

App store listing assets: although alpha can run on the web PWA alone, the Flutter mobile client in progress will eventually need standard Play Store / App Store assets — app icon, feature graphic, 3 to 5 screenshots per platform showing the owner dashboard, tenant payment screen, and M-Pesa confirmation, a short (80-character) and long description, and a content rating questionnaire response. None of this blocks web-based alpha and should be sequenced after mobile parity work, not before.

Marketing/landing page: there is currently no landing page in the repository — `app/components/KodaraApp.tsx` is the workspace itself, with no pre-auth marketing surface. A minimal landing page is needed before any paid or broad-channel acquisition (it is not needed for the warm-network/WhatsApp-group alpha recruitment in Section 2, which can run on a one-page pitch deck or direct demo instead). Positioning should lead with the reconciliation pain point identified in Section 2 — "stop manually matching M-Pesa messages to tenants" — rather than generic "property management software" language, since the latter is already crowded (Nyumba Zetu, RentSawa, Bomahut, Pangoni, pms.co.ke, Blocks PMS all use that framing). Copy direction: concrete, numbers-led, Kenya-specific (KES amounts, named Nairobi areas, M-Pesa terminology like till/paybill rather than generic "payment gateway"), and short — the buyer is a busy landlord checking their phone, not reading a SaaS feature comparison page.

Week 1 alpha metrics to watch, in priority order: (1) activation rate — percentage of invited owners who complete property + unit + tenant + M-Pesa setup (Section 1a) within 48 hours of signup, since this is the step most likely to silently fail; (2) time-to-first-payment — elapsed time from tenant invite SMS to a completed M-Pesa STK push payment, both in aggregate and broken out per landlord, since a landlord whose tenants take weeks to pay through the app hasn't actually solved their reconciliation problem yet; (3) payment success rate — percentage of initiated STK pushes that complete versus fail/timeout/cancel, cross-checked against the Safaricom portal per FINAL_SETUP.md's reconciliation requirement, since a low success rate here is the single fastest way to lose alpha landlord trust; (4) tenant verification completion rate — percentage of invited tenants who complete phone OTP verification at all, since a tenant who never verifies never becomes a payer regardless of how good the payment flow is; (5) support-channel volume and theme, read qualitatively every day during week 1, since at 15 to 20 landlords the team can and should read every single message rather than wait for aggregated dashboards.
